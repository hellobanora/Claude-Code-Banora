Seasonal Health
Why Cold Weather Makes Everything Stiffer
As temperatures drop across the Tweed and Gold Coast, we see a noticeable increase in patients presenting with joint stiffness, lower back pain, and reduced mobility. It’s not your imagination — cooler air genuinely reduces blood flow to muscles and tendons, making them tighter and more prone to strain.
Combined with less physical activity (those morning walks get harder to commit to!), your spine loses the muscular support it relies on. Here’s what to do about it:
Your Winter Spine
Survival Guide
1
Warm up first. 5 minutes before any activity — even gardening or housework.
2
Keep moving daily. A brisk 20-minute walk maintains spinal mobility through winter.
3
Layer your lower back. Cold muscles tighten further — keep your core warm.
4
Stretch before bed. Prevents overnight stiffening that hits you in the morning.
5
Stay hydrated. We drink less in winter but spinal discs still need water.
 
Education Spotlight
Understanding Your Spine: The Basics
Your spine isn’t just one bone — it’s an intricate system of 33 vertebrae, shock-absorbing discs, and a network of nerves that controls virtually every function in your body. When one region is out of alignment, it affects the entire chain.
That’s why a “simple” neck issue can sometimes show up as headaches, shoulder tension, or even tingling in your hands. And as disc degeneration progresses gradually over time, early intervention makes a real difference.
The Spine & Disc Degeneration
Patient Education
i
Understanding how your spine works helps you make better decisions about your health. Ask Dr James or Dr Paul about any of these areas at your next visit.
Cervical (C1–C7)
Controls your head, neck, shoulders and arms. Misalignment here often causes headaches and arm tingling.
Thoracic (T1–T12)
Supports your ribcage and protects vital organs. Desk posture hits this region hardest.
Lumbar (L1–L5)
Bears most of your body weight. The most common site for disc bulges and lower back pain.
Sacrum & Coccyx
The foundation of your spine. Connects to your pelvis and provides base stability.
